Data Profile

Information about the training data (Oxford-IIIT Pet Dataset) used in our pet classification model.

Class Balance

The Oxford-IIIT Pet Dataset offers a wonderfully diverse snapshot of our favorite companion animals, featuring 25 dog breeds totaling 4,978 images and 12 cat breeds comprising 2,371 images. This numerical imbalance (dogs outnumber cats by about 2:1) reflects the real-world reality that there are more recognized dog breeds than cat breeds. Each individual breed is well-represented with approximately 200 images per category (with just a few exceptions like the British Shorthair at 184 and Staffordshire Bull Terrier at 189). While this dataset captures the wide variety of our four-legged friends, it's worth noting that the dog/cat imbalance may cause our classification model to perform slightly better with dogs than cats simply because more canine examples were seen during training. Whether you have a tiny Chihuahua, a fluffy Persian, or a majestic Great Pyrenees, our pet classification model has been trained to recognize your beloved companion!

Label Noise

The training dataset may contain some incorrectly labeled images, or label noise. Even experienced animal lovers and veterinarians sometimes struggle to distinguish between visually similar breeds like Basset Hounds and Beagles (both with droopy ears and similar coloration), British Shorthairs and Russian Blues (both blue-gray cats with similar body types), or Ragdolls and Birmans (both with pointed coloration and semi-long fur). These challenging distinctions can introduce incorrect labels in the training data that the model then learns and reproduces. When our model occasionally mistakes one breed for a similar-looking breed, it may reflect these challenges for human experts.

Bias Assessment

While our pet recognition model aims to accurately identify a wide variety of dog and cat breeds, there are some important limitations to be aware of. The numerical imbalance in our dataset (4,978 dog images versus 2,371 cat images) may cause the model to perform better on dog recognition simply because it has seen more canine examples during training. Similarly, breeds with distinctive features, like the wrinkly Shar-Pei or hairless Sphynx cat, may be identified with greater accuracy than breeds sharing similar characteristics, such as the various terrier breeds, which can sometimes be confused with one another. The dataset includes pets photographed in various lighting conditions, backgrounds, and photo styles—from professional portraits to casual snapshots—but in-focus images will produce the best results as this better reflects the training data. Color patterns can also affect recognition—cats with tabby patterns might occasionally be misclassified among similar-patterned breeds. Our model may struggle with mixed-breed pets, unusual angles, or pets wearing accessories; providing clear, well-lit photos of your pet's face will help achieve the most accurate results.
